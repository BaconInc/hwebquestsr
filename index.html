About

Before the Holocaust

Before the holocaust, Solomon lived in Praga, which is located in Warsaw Poland.Solomon was a furrier, someone who works with furs and jackets. Solomon himself had a family of 78, but he only lived with 7 others: his two parents Jacob and Toby, his two brothers Moshie and Baruch, and his three sisters Sarah, Livka, and Leah.

In the ghetto

While in the ghetto itself, his father, mother, and older sister were killed. First his mother and sister died when a Nazi demanded all of the gold. When they told him that they did not have any, he shot the two of them. Then, his father died when a Jewish officer pointed him out to a German. In order to get food for the family, he bought food from two boys who smuggled it in. After hearing of this, the officer shot him in the back. Solomon's four other siblings were sent to Treblinka, and he never saw them again. A friend had told Solomon that he had seen his sister in a shop three kilometers away, and when he left the ghetto his sister wasn't there. To top it off, the Warsaw Ghetto Uprising was the day after, so he was unable to re-enter the ghetto.

In the Camps

Solomon's life in both Auschwitz and Majdanek

Life in Majdanek

Upon arrival to Majdanek, all of Solomon's clothes had been taken from him. In their place he was given black striped clothing, and wooden shoes. He was then assigned to Barrack 21. There he met a doctor from Paris who asked him if he was okay. When he told the doctor that he had been shot in the ankle, the doctor then took out a pocket knife and helped Solomon. Later in his time at Majdanek, when he was working, someone had been smoking simply on a piece of paper to create the feeling that he had been smoking. However, a guard thought that a prison had actually been smoking, and demanded that the man should be pointed out. When no one said anything, he took ten men up to a bench to be hanged. Solomon was one of the ten. Luckily, mere seconds before he was killed, another guard ran up and yelled at the first guard, telling him that a prison transfer was to occur, and that he could not send dead prisoners. Solomon was one of the prisoners who was to be transferred. Sadly, he was being transferred to Auschwitz.

Life in Auschwitz

When Solomon arrived at Auschwitz Birkenau, he was given a shower, a haircut, and then was branded. Then number he was given was 128232. These numbers individually add up to 18. In Hebrew, numbers correspond to letters, and 18 means life. Solomon was then assigned to building railroad tracks. One day Solomon fell down and could not get up. This led to him being transferred to the Auschwitz - 1 camp: the death camp. There he was assigned to Barrack 6. In the barrack, someone asked him if he knew a specific friend from the Warsaw ghetto, Solomon responded yes, and that he had worked with him. After even saying his nickname, the barrack inmates believed him, and gave him great hospitality. After finding out that he had been assigned to the coal mines, one of the deadliest jobs, they bribed the Capo, or the Barrack Guard, to change his job. The Capo said yes. Solomon was then sent to work with his barrack inmates, where they farmed sand and brought it in a wheelbarrel to Auschwitz Birkenau. There they layed it in holes over the ashes from the crematorium. Later, SOlomon was asked to make a hat for the Capo, for he had been a furrier in the ghetto. After doing so, the Capo loved Solomon, and never hit him ever again. Auschwitz was later liquidated, and Solomon left on January 18th, 1945. Sadly, the Russians liberated the camp on the 27th. Yet, in the end, Solomon was liberated by the Americans after they stopped his train and set all of the prisoners free.

Twice a day we carried sand to Birkenau to cover the ashes of the dead. The sand was to cover the ashes that came from the crematoria. I did this for more than a year.
